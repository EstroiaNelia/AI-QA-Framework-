## Question 1

**Question:** What is the definition of artificial intelligence (AI) today?

**Correct answer:**  
Artificial intelligence is the capability of an engineered system to acquire, process, create, and apply knowledge and skills.

**Syllabus rationale:**  
AI is not defined only as the imitation of human beings. The important point is that an engineered system can use knowledge and skills to perform tasks that require intelligent behaviour.

---

## Question 2

**Question:** Why is the "brute force" approach implemented in the Deep Blue system not considered true artificial intelligence?

**Correct answer:**  
The brute force approach used by Deep Blue is not considered true artificial intelligence because the system did not learn from data.

**Syllabus rationale:**  
Modern AI-based systems, especially machine learning systems, are associated with learning from data and improving or adapting through training. Deep Blue relied mainly on programmed search and evaluation rather than learning from data.

## Question 4

**Question:** Which statement best captures the distinction between the expert systems of the 1970s and 1980s and modern AI?

**Correct answer:**  
Expert systems relied on fixed rules and lacked self-learning capabilities, whereas modern AI systems can learn from data and improve over time.

**Syllabus rationale:**  
Expert systems used human-defined rules and knowledge bases. Modern AI, especially machine learning, builds models from data and may improve through training, retraining, or adaptation.

---

## Question 5

**Question:** Which scenario best reflects the concept of the "AI Effect"?

**Correct answer:**  
The scenario that best reflects the AI Effect is one where a capability is initially considered AI, but later stops being perceived as true AI once a more advanced AI capability becomes available or once the original capability becomes familiar.

**Syllabus rationale:**  
The AI Effect describes the shifting perception of what is considered artificial intelligence. Capabilities once regarded as AI may later be seen as ordinary software once they become common or better understood.

**Syllabus rationale:**  
Narrow AI is designed to perform specific tasks within a limited context. Current real-world AI systems are generally examples of narrow AI.

## Question 7

**Question:** What is the main difference between narrow AI and general AI?

**Correct answer:**  
Narrow AI has limited cognitive abilities for specific tasks, while general AI would have wide-ranging cognitive abilities similar to humans.

**Syllabus rationale:**  
Narrow AI is specialized and task-focused. General AI would be able to understand, learn, and apply knowledge across a broad range of tasks without needing to be retrained for each new task.

## Quick revision rules

- Narrow AI performs specific tasks in limited contexts.
- General AI would perform many intellectual tasks like a human.
- Super AI would surpass human intelligence.
- Conventional software follows explicit programmed logic.
- ML systems learn patterns from data.
- AI-based systems may be less explainable than conventional systems.
- GPUs and AI-specific hardware are useful because ML workloads are highly computational and parallel.
- Pre-trained models reduce training effort, but they do not remove the need for validation, documentation, and data-related work.
- Transfer learning is more effective when the original and new tasks are similar.
- Bias mitigation starts with representative data and appropriate testing.
- XAI supports trust, transparency, interpretability, and regulatory needs.
- Safety means the system is expected not to cause harm.
